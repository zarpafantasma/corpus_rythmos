## 4. Red Team Advisory: Engineering Constraints & Biological Hazards

Applying extreme topological gradients to human biology introduces unprecedented physics hazards that standard medical equipment designers have never faced. 

### 4.1 Topological Boundary Shear (The Guillotine Effect)
The most severe risk of the Stasis Chamber is the boundary layer between the $\alpha=50$ field (inside the bed) and the $\alpha \approx 1$ field (the hospital room). 
If a doctor attempts to reach their hand through a sharp topological boundary to apply a tourniquet, the blood in their forearm would be flowing 50 times faster than the blood in their fingertips. This violent differential would cause immediate cellular shearing, effectively acting as an invisible guillotine.



**Mitigation:** The Stasis Chamber cannot have an "open door" while active. It requires a **Gradient Transition Lock**—an intermediate zone that smoothly ramps the $\alpha$ value down before it meets normal space. Furthermore, surgical intervention *cannot* happen while the field is active; the field must be powered down sequentially to bring the patient back to normal temporal flow before scalpels breach the skin.

### 4.2 Power Density and Thermal Runaway
Maintaining an $\alpha = 50$ static field over a volume large enough to encompass an adult human requires sustained energy injection far beyond the 50W used in the Mark 1 prototype. 
The piezoelectric array required for a human-sized chamber would consume megawatts of power, generating massive thermal exhaust.
**Mitigation:** Hospital-grade Stasis Chambers will require dedicated industrial three-phase power lines and active liquid-helium cooling jackets to prevent the PZT-5H actuators from depolarizing and instantly collapsing the temporal bubble. Portable ambulance units would be limited to much lower gradients (e.g., $\alpha = 5$) due to battery and cooling limitations, offering a 5x time extension rather than 50x.
